2. Part 1: Selfie: /h3>

Discussion

The second portrait (Fig 2) is clearly more flattering. The distortion in the first selfie (Fig 1) is caused by **perspective**, not the lens itself. When the camera is very close, the relative distance between my nose and my ears is significant compared to the overall distance to the camera. This exaggerates the features closest to the camera. By stepping back and zooming in, I decreased this relative distance, resulting in a portrait with more natural, compressed facial proportions.

3. Part 2: Architectural Perspective Compression

Discussion

The telephoto image (Fig 3) appears "flattened." Objects in the foreground and background seem closer together than they actually are. This is **perspective compression**. Because the camera is far away, the large distances between objects along the path are small relative to the camera's distance. In the wide-angle shot (Fig 4), the camera is closer, which exaggerates the sense of depth and makes the path seem much longer.

4. Part 3: The Dolly Zoom

Discussion

This effect works by combining two opposite actions. The physical movement of the camera (the "dolly") changes the perspective, altering the relative size and position of background objects. Simultaneously, adjusting the zoom (focal length) counteracts this movement to keep the main subject at a constant size. The result is a visually unsettling effect where the world seems to warp around a static subject.
